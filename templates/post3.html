Dealing With Picky Eaters


You Are Not Alone

Although you might feel isolated in the challenge of introducing new foods to your child, rest assured that many parents share this struggle.

Research indicates that up to half of parents with preschool-age children encounter picky eating behaviors.

Navigating the realm of picky eaters can be exasperating, particularly when you're uncertain about effective and safe methods to broaden your child's palate.

Moreover, children who stick to a limited array of foods may miss out on essential nutrients crucial for their healthy growth and development.

However, there's promising news. Numerous evidence-based strategies exist to encourage your child to explore, embrace, and even relish new foods.

Here are 16 useful tips to try if your child is a picky eater:

  • 1. Create a Fun Mealtime Atmosphere

    Ensure mealtime is enjoyable and stress-free. Let kids explore foods by touching and tasting without frustration. Arrange food into fun shapes to make mealtime more appealing. Keep meals under 30 minutes to avoid prolonged eating sessions.

  • 2. Get Kids Involved in Cooking and Meal Planning

    Engage children in cooking, shopping, and meal planning. Allow them to pick healthy items at the store and assist with age-appropriate kitchen tasks. This involvement increases their interest in food and likelihood to try new dishes.

  • 3. Be Creative with Food Presentation

    Make foods visually appealing and fun to eat. Add leafy greens to colorful smoothies and incorporate chopped vegetables into child-friendly dishes like pasta, pizza, and soup. Use cookie cutters to create fun shapes from fruits and vegetables.

  • 4. Maintain Control Over Meals

    As a parent, stay in charge of meal choices. Serve the same meal to the entire family, combining new foods with familiar favorites. Avoid catering to picky eaters by preparing separate dishes.

  • 5. Start with Small Portions

    Introduce new foods with small portions to avoid overwhelming your child. Serve a small amount before their favorite dishes and gradually increase the portion size as they become more comfortable with the new food.

  • 6. Cut Out Distractions During Mealtime

    Create a focused eating environment by turning off the TV and removing toys and electronics. Seat children at the dining table to help them concentrate on their meals.

  • 7. Model Healthy Eating Habits

    Be a role model by making healthy food choices. Children are more likely to try new foods when they see others eating them. Increase your consumption of nutritious foods in front of your child to encourage them to follow suit.

  • 8. Use Positive Reinforcement

    Avoid using unhealthy foods as rewards. Instead, use non-food rewards like verbal praise, stickers, pencils, extra playtime, or choosing a favorite game after dinner to encourage trying new foods.

  • 9. Understand Taste and Texture Preferences

    Consider your child's taste and texture preferences when introducing new foods. Offer new foods with similar textures to those they already like. For example, if they prefer crunchy foods, offer raw vegetables; if they prefer soft foods, try cooked vegetables.

  • 10. Be Patient with Picky Eaters

    Patience is essential when dealing with picky eaters. Most children outgrow picky eating within a few years. Avoid pressuring them to eat, as it can increase resistance. Consistent encouragement will help expand their food preferences over time.

  • 11. Limit Unhealthy Snacks

    Reduce unhealthy snacking to ensure children are hungry at mealtimes. Offer healthy meals and snacks every 2-3 hours. Serve filling beverages or foods like milk or soup at the end of meals.

  • 12. Expose Kids to New Foods Regularly

    Keep offering new foods, even if they are initially rejected. Children may need multiple exposures before accepting new foods. Pair a small amount of the new food with a familiar favorite, but don’t force them to try it.

  • 13. Encourage Mindful Eating

    Teach children to be mindful of their hunger and fullness cues. Ask questions like “Does your tummy have room for another bite?” to help them become more aware of their appetite. Respect their sense of fullness and avoid urging them to eat past it.

  • 14. Consider Food Intolerances and Allergies

    Monitor your child for potential food intolerances or allergies. Keep a food journal to note what they refuse to eat and any symptoms they may experience. Consult a pediatrician if you suspect an intolerance or allergy.

  • 15. Encourage Social Eating

    Encourage your child to eat with friends who are adventurous eaters. Watching peers try new foods can motivate your child to do the same. Serve a mix of new foods and familiar favorites during these social meals.

  • 16. Seek Professional Help if Needed

    Consult a healthcare professional if you notice signs of serious eating issues, such as difficulty swallowing, slow growth, vomiting, or signs of autism. Pediatricians and dietitians can provide guidance and support for managing picky eating behavior.

  • Conclusion

    Encouraging your toddler to embrace vegetables may pose a challenge, but it's certainly achievable.

    If your child rejects a vegetable initially, don't be discouraged. Keep reintroducing it, perhaps in different preparations or alongside favorite dishes. Engaging your kids in shopping for groceries or cooking, and incorporating vegetables into familiar meals they love, can make a significant difference.

    Be patient as it might take a while for your toddler to develop a liking for vegetables, even if you're implementing various strategies. Remember, many parents encounter hurdles when it comes to feeding their children. Every meal presents a fresh chance to make progress!
